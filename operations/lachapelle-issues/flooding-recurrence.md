Grievance: Chronic Flooding Recurrence and Sabotage of Tenant-Led Mitigation

Status: ACTIVE / RECURRING | Severity: High (Asset Damage & Safety Hazard)

Description of the Flooding Failure

The facility at 130 W Lachapelle suffers from systemic drainage failures, particularly around the rear walls. For several years, 10BitWorks members maintained their own effective, albeit non-professional, flood mitigation infrastructure. During the 2025/2026 renovation and plumbing additions, the Landlord's crew actively removed these protections and failed to provide a professional alternative, leading to the immediate and recurring flooding of the workshop’s specialized ceramics area.

Detailed Evidence & Verification

1. The Destruction of the "Joe Rogers" Shielding

Joe Rogers installed a large piece of plastic shielding and a secondary diversion barrier behind the building that remained in place for nearly five years. This system diverted San Antonio's heavy rainwater away from the rear door and the ceramics workshop floor much more effectively than the Property Manager’s lackluster mitigation.

Removal: During the installation of the "Triple Daisy-Chain" plumbing for new tenants, the Landlord’s crew (Property Manager Ryan) removed this entire mitigation system to facilitate their own work.

Neglect: The crew failed to reinstall the shielding or provide any professional-grade replacement, despite the Board’s explicit warnings that the door would fail during the next rain event.

2. Recent Incident: The April 18, 2026 Flood

The predictable consequences of the Landlord's actions were documented during the Field Recording walkthrough on April 19, 2026.

The Storm: A significant storm occurred on the night of April 18, 2026.

Direct Observation: "Stormed last night and within a few hours... the water had already reached to the ceramics room. So that's why Joe Rogers, why we had that big piece of plastic that was back there before they built that that they took out." [Field Recording]

A much more serious “Flash Flood Warning” storm on May 2nd created the same issue, with water reaching nearly every part of the back hallway, and crawling through the ceramics room nearly far enough to reach the front door.

3. Abandonment of Repairs

Property manager Ryan reportedly acknowledged the flooding risk but abandoned the project before a professional fix could be implemented.

Direct Quote: "They removed the shielding that we put in when they did the plumbing and then never put it back... maybe they had plans and then Alex just stopped paying him, so he just stopped working." [Field Recording]

Consequences & Safety Risks

Electrocution Hazard: Standing water on a workshop floor containing high-voltage (240V) ceramics kilns and industrial machinery creates an unacceptable life-safety risk for members.

Asset Damage: Recurring moisture in the ceramics area has damaged member projects and accelerated the corrosion of specialized kiln components.

Slum Lord Pattern: The Landlord’s decision to stop paying his contractors mid-repair has left the Tenant in a worse position than before the "improvement" began.

Lease Leverage & Actionable Breach

This failure constitutes a direct breach of the Landlord's duty to maintain a weather-tight building shell.

Section 13 (Landlord's Repairs): The Landlord is responsible for the "Roof and Exterior Walls." The failure of the rear door seal and the active destruction of flood shielding falls squarely under this mandate.

Actionable Claim: 10BitWorks could seek a total reimbursement of $1,500 for labor and materials to replace the flood shielding, plus any documented equipment loss from the various floods.

Demand for Remedy: We may issue a formal notice that the Landlord must provide a professional, permanent drainage solution for the rear of the building within 30 days or accept an immediate, mutual lease termination.
